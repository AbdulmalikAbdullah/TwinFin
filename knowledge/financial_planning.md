# Financial Planning: Turning Money Into Decisions

A financial plan is not a spreadsheet. It is a small set of rules that let you answer "should I
do this?" quickly and consistently, without renegotiating your values every time a salesman is
standing in front of you.

## Know your four numbers

Everything below is built on four figures. If you cannot recite them, you do not have a plan.

1. **Monthly surplus** = salary - living expenses - debt payments. This is the only money that
   is actually free.
2. **Emergency fund target** = 6 x monthly expenses. Your floor.
3. **Liquid savings.** What you could reach this week.
4. **Debt-to-income ratio** = total monthly debt payments / salary. Under 15% is comfortable.
   Above 33% is a warning sign, and SAMA's regulations cap total deductions for consumer
   finance at a share of salary precisely because high DTI destroys households.

## A financial health score

A useful way to hold all four in one place is a single 0–100 score built from weighted
components: savings rate (how much of your income survives the month), emergency fund coverage
(how many months of expenses you hold in cash), debt-to-income (lower is better), and progress
toward your named goals. No single component tells the truth on its own   a high salary with a
thin buffer and heavy financing is a fragile position, and the score is designed to make that
fragility visible in one number.

The score's real value is comparative. The interesting question is never "what is my score
today?" but **"what will this decision do to it?"** A purchase that drops your score by two
points is noise. One that drops it by twenty has told you something.

## Vision 2030 context

Saudi Arabia's Financial Sector Development Program has pushed hard on household savings, and
the Financial Literacy Entrepreneurship Program exists because the national savings rate has
historically been low relative to income. The structural picture matters for you personally:

- **No income tax** means high take-home pay and an unusually high *potential* savings rate.
  The constraint is behavioural, not arithmetic.
- **VAT at 15%** applies to most purchases, so the sticker price is not the price. A 120,000 SAR
  car quote should be checked for whether VAT is included.
- **End-of-service benefits** are real but should not be treated as retirement provision, and
  **GOSI** contributions are a floor, not a plan.

## Plan around goals, not products

Name your goals, attach a number and a date to each, and fund them in order. "Buy a car
(~120,000 SAR) within 12 months" is a plan. "Invest more" is not.

Then apply one test before every large decision:

> Does this keep my emergency fund intact, keep my surplus above 10% of salary, and leave my
> named goals on schedule?

Three yeses and it is a decision you can make quickly. Any no, and the honest answer is not
"never"   it is **"not like this."** Wait and save, buy a cheaper version, or finance it with
your eyes open and accept the cost. What separates people who build wealth from people who
merely earn well is that they run this test *before* they sign, not after.
