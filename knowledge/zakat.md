# Zakat on Savings and How Big Purchases Change It

Zakat is the third pillar of Islam and, for individuals, a 2.5% annual obligation on
qualifying wealth that has been held for a full lunar year (a *hawl*).

## What you pay zakat on

Zakat is due on **zakatable assets** — broadly, wealth that is held or grows — not on
everything you own:

- **Cash** in hand and in bank accounts. Yes.
- **Gold and silver.** Yes.
- **Shares and investment funds** held for trading, at market value. Yes. (Shares held
  long-term for dividends are commonly zakated on the underlying zakatable assets, but many
  scholars accept the simpler full-market-value approach.)
- **Money owed to you** that you reasonably expect to recover. Yes.
- **Your home, your car, your furniture, your phone.** No — personal-use assets are exempt,
  no matter how expensive.
- **Debts you owe** that are due now are generally deducted from the base.

## The nisab threshold

Zakat is only due if your zakatable wealth stays at or above the **nisab** for the full lunar
year. Nisab is the value of 85 grams of gold (or 595 grams of silver; the gold standard is more
commonly used in the Gulf). At roughly 300 SAR per gram, that is approximately **25,500 SAR** —
but gold moves, so check the current figure at the time of calculation rather than relying on a
number from last year.

## The calculation

    zakatable wealth = cash + gold + tradeable investments + recoverable debts - immediate debts
    zakat due        = zakatable wealth x 2.5%   (if >= nisab, held for one lunar year)

**Example.** Saad holds 250,000 SAR in cash and a 40,000 SAR investment account.

    zakatable base = 250,000 + 40,000 = 290,000 SAR
    zakat due      = 290,000 x 2.5% = 7,250 SAR/year

## How a large purchase affects your zakat base

This is the part people miss. **Converting cash into a personal-use asset removes it from the
zakat base.** If Saad spends 120,000 SAR of cash on a car for personal use:

    new zakatable base = 290,000 - 120,000 = 170,000 SAR
    new zakat due      = 170,000 x 2.5% = 4,250 SAR/year

His zakat obligation falls by 3,000 SAR a year. This is a real, arithmetic consequence — but
it is **not a reason to buy the car.** Spending 120,000 SAR to reduce a payment by 3,000 SAR is
a terrible trade if you did not want the car anyway, and zakat is an act of worship, not a tax
to be minimised. Treat the change as information, not motivation.

Two related notes:

- **Financing instead of paying cash keeps the cash in your base.** If Saad finances the car
  through murabaha, his 250,000 SAR stays in the bank and stays zakatable. The monthly
  installments that are *due* can be deducted as they fall due, but the lump sum remains.
- **Money earmarked for a future purchase is still zakatable.** Saving up for a car does not
  exempt the savings. Intention does not remove the obligation; only the actual purchase does.

Pay zakat on the same lunar date each year so the hawl is easy to track, and keep the
calculation simple and honest rather than optimised.
