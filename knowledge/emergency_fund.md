# The Emergency Fund

An emergency fund is cash you can reach today, held for events you did not plan: job loss,
a medical bill, an urgent flight home, a car that fails its inspection. It is not an
investment and it is not a savings goal. It is insurance you pay yourself.

## How much is enough

The standard target is **six months of essential expenses**. Note the word *expenses*, not
*salary* — you are funding your survival costs, not your lifestyle. If your monthly outgoings
are 9,000 SAR, your target is:

    6 x 9,000 = 54,000 SAR

Adjust the multiple to your situation:

- **3–4 months** if you are a government employee or have a very secure long-tenure contract,
  no dependents, and family who could house you.
- **6 months** for most private-sector employees. This is the default.
- **9–12 months** if you are a contractor, commission-based, an expat whose residency is tied
  to the job, or the sole earner for a family. In the Kingdom, losing a job as an expat can
  start a short clock on your iqama — cash buys you time to find the next role rather than
  accepting the first one offered.

## Where to keep it

Liquidity beats yield. The fund should sit somewhere you can access within a day or two:

- A separate savings account at your bank, deliberately *not* the account your card is linked
  to. Friction is a feature.
- A **murabaha-based savings product** or Shariah-compliant deposit account if you want the
  balance to grow without earning riba. Saudi banks offer these under names like "savings
  account (Islamic)" and returns are modest — that is fine and expected.

Do **not** keep it in equities, in a company you cannot exit quickly, or lent to a relative.
An investment account that is down 20% on the day you need it is not an emergency fund.

## The rule that protects everything else

**A discretionary purchase should never take your liquid savings below the emergency fund
target.** This is the single most useful line in personal finance. It converts a vague
feeling of "can I afford this?" into a testable statement:

    savings after purchase >= 6 x monthly expenses ?

If the answer is no, the purchase is not unaffordable in the abstract — it is unaffordable
*right now, in cash*. The correct responses are to wait and save, buy a cheaper version, or
finance it deliberately and accept the profit cost. What you must not do is dip into the
buffer and tell yourself you will "put it back next month." The month you need the fund is
never the month you expected to need it.

## Rebuilding after you use it

If an emergency does draw the fund down, treat rebuilding it as your top financial priority —
above investing, above goal saving, above upgrading anything. Redirect the full monthly
surplus until the buffer is whole again, then resume normal allocation.
