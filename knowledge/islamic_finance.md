# Islamic Finance: The Contracts You Will Actually Meet

Islamic finance is built on a short list of prohibitions and a long list of contracts designed
to work around them. For a salaried person in Saudi Arabia, five contracts cover almost
everything you will be offered at a bank.

## The three prohibitions

- **Riba (interest).** Money may not earn money for the mere passage of time. A lender may
  profit from trade or from sharing risk, not from charging for delay.
- **Gharar (excessive uncertainty).** Contracts must have known subject matter, known price,
  and known terms. This is why conventional insurance and speculative derivatives are
  problematic.
- **Maysir (gambling).** Wealth transferred on pure chance is prohibited.

The underlying principle is that **profit must be tied to risk**. If you take none of the risk
of ownership, you have no claim on the return.

## Murabaha — cost-plus sale

The bank buys the asset and resells it to you at a disclosed markup, payable in installments.
Ownership genuinely passes to the bank first, which is what distinguishes it from a loan. The
markup is fixed at signing and cannot grow. This is the workhorse of Saudi retail finance: car
finance, appliance finance, and most personal finance products are murabaha, frequently
structured through commodity trades (tawarruq).

Because the total is fixed on day one, murabaha is predictable — but it is not free. A 5% flat
profit rate on 120,000 SAR over 36 months costs you 6,000 SAR.

## Ijara — leasing

The bank owns the asset and leases it to you for a rental payment. Common in home finance
(ijara muntahia bittamleek — a lease ending in ownership) and in some car products. You are
paying for *use*, and the bank carries ownership risk, which is what makes the rental
permissible.

## Mudaraba and musharaka — partnership

**Mudaraba:** one party provides capital, the other provides expertise; profit is shared on an
agreed ratio and loss falls on the capital provider. This is the basis of most Islamic savings
and investment accounts — your deposit is capital, the bank is the manager, and your return is
a profit share, not interest.

**Musharaka:** both parties contribute capital and share profit and loss. Used in home finance
as diminishing musharaka, where you progressively buy out the bank's share.

## Takaful — cooperative insurance

Participants contribute to a common pool that pays members' claims; surplus may be returned to
participants. It avoids gharar by being framed as mutual donation rather than a bet on an
uncertain event. All insurance in the Kingdom is required to be takaful-structured.

## Practical guidance

- **Do not assume a product is compliant because the bank's name sounds Islamic.** Ask for the
  Shariah board's approval and read what contract you are actually signing.
- **Compare on total cost, not on the label.** A murabaha with a high markup can cost more than
  a cheap conventional loan. Compliance and value are separate questions and you should satisfy
  both.
- **The best Islamic finance decision is usually to need less finance.** No contract is cheaper
  than paying cash, and no structure makes an unaffordable purchase affordable. Islamic finance
  changes the *form* of the obligation, not the fact that a future month of your income is now
  spoken for.
