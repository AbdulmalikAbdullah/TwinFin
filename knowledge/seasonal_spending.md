# Seasonal Spending in Saudi Arabia

Household spending in Saudi Arabia is not flat across the year. It rises sharply in predictable seasons, and families who plan ahead avoid debt, protect their savings, and keep their emergency fund intact. The best tool for seasonal costs is a "sinking fund": a fixed amount set aside monthly, months before the season arrives.

## Ramadan
Grocery, iftar hosting, and charity spending typically rise 20–50% during Ramadan compared to a normal month. For a household spending 9,000 SAR per month, expect roughly 1,500–3,000 SAR in extra costs. Tips: start a Ramadan sinking fund 3 months in advance, plan iftar hosting per week instead of improvising, and set your charity (sadaqah) budget before the month begins.

## Eid al-Fitr
Immediately after Ramadan, families spend on new clothes, gifts, Eidiya for children, and travel to visit relatives. A typical extra cost is 1,000–3,000 SAR. Tips: buy clothes before the last-week price surge, and agree on an Eidiya budget per child in advance.

## Eid al-Adha and Hajj season
The udhiyah (sacrifice) typically costs 1,500–2,500 SAR, in addition to hosting and travel. Tips: reserve the udhiyah amount early in the year as part of the annual budget, and treat it as a fixed yearly obligation, not a surprise.

## Summer travel season (June–August)
Flight and hotel prices peak in summer. A family vacation commonly costs 8,000–20,000 SAR depending on destination. Tips: set a fixed travel budget before choosing the destination, book 2–3 months early, and never finance a vacation with debt — if the sinking fund is not enough, choose a cheaper trip.

## Back-to-school (August–September)
School fees, uniforms, supplies, and transportation renewals hit in one narrow window. Families commonly face 1,500–5,000 SAR in extra costs per child (much more with private school fees). Tips: divide known school fees by 12 and set them aside monthly; buy supplies in early August before prices rise.

## Zakat hawl (annual zakat due date)
Zakat of 2.5% becomes due on eligible savings once a full Hijri year (hawl) passes. Someone with 250,000 SAR in zakat-eligible savings owes about 6,250 SAR at the hawl. Tips: know your hawl date, reserve roughly one-twelfth of the expected zakat every month, and remember that a large purchase shortly before the hawl reduces the zakat base, while the same purchase after the hawl does not — timing major spending around the hawl date is a legitimate planning consideration.

## Important note on dates
Hijri-linked seasons (Ramadan, both Eids, and the zakat hawl) move about 11 days earlier each Gregorian year, so exact dates should always be checked against the current calendar.

## Why seasons matter for financial decisions
A large discretionary purchase made right before an expensive season (Ramadan, back-to-school, summer travel) is riskier than the same purchase in a quiet month, because the season's extra costs and the purchase both hit savings and cash flow at the same time. Planning purchases for quiet months, and pre-saving for known seasons, keeps liquidity stable across the year.
