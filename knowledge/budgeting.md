# Budgeting in Saudi Arabia

Budgeting is the habit of telling every riyal where to go before the month starts. For a
salaried professional in Riyadh or Jeddah, the biggest structural cost is almost always
housing, followed by transport and food. A budget that ignores those three will not survive
contact with reality.

## The 50/30/20 rule, adapted for Saudi salaries

The classic split is 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings. In Saudi Arabia this needs two
adjustments:

1. **Annual rent is paid up front.** Most landlords still ask for the full year, or two
   payments of six months. If your rent is 42,000 SAR a year, you are not paying 3,500 SAR
   a month — you are paying 42,000 SAR in one shock. Treat rent as a *sinking fund*: move
   3,500 SAR into a separate account every month so the annual bill is already funded when
   it lands. People who skip this step end up financing rent with a personal loan, which is
   one of the most expensive mistakes a Saudi salary earner can make.
2. **Aim higher than 20% if you can.** Because income tax is not deducted from salaries in
   the Kingdom, take-home pay is high relative to gross. A saver on 18,000 SAR/month with
   9,000 SAR of expenses can realistically save 35–45% of income. Anchoring to 20% leaves a
   lot of money on the table.

## Build the budget from your actual cash flow

Start from net monthly surplus:

    surplus = salary - living expenses - debt payments

Only the surplus is genuinely available for goals. A common error is to plan against
salary rather than surplus, which quietly double-counts money that is already committed to a
personal loan or a car installment. If your surplus is thin — under 10% of salary — you are
one broken air conditioner away from borrowing.

## Categories that quietly leak money

- **Subscriptions.** Streaming, gym, cloud storage, delivery memberships. Individually small,
  collectively 300–800 SAR/month for many young professionals. Audit them twice a year.
- **Food delivery.** Apps make it frictionless. A 45 SAR order four times a week is 720
  SAR/month, roughly a third of a typical food budget.
- **Fuel and car upkeep.** Petrol is cheap by global standards but insurance, tyres,
  registration (istimara) and maintenance are not. Budget an annual figure and divide by 12.

## Review cadence

Check the budget monthly, and rebuild it whenever income or a fixed cost changes by more
than 10%. Before any purchase above roughly one month of expenses, run the numbers first:
what does this do to my surplus, my emergency fund, and my timeline to the goals I already
said mattered? A purchase that survives that test is a decision. One that does not is an
impulse wearing a decision's clothes.
